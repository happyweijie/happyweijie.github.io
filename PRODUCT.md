# Product

## Register

brand

## Platform

web

## Users

Recruiters and hiring managers screening Wei Jie for internships and early-career software roles. They arrive from a resume link or LinkedIn, skim in under a minute, and are deciding whether this candidate is worth a closer look. They see hundreds of CS student portfolios; most blur together.

## Product Purpose

Wei Jie's personal portfolio site (happyweijie.github.io). It exists to turn a resume-line visit into a durable impression: success is a recruiter leaving with a clear, memorable sense that this candidate is distinct from every other CS undergrad they screened that day.

## Positioning

A builder who ships. Not a student who lists coursework — someone with internships, hackathon wins, and working tools with real users. Every section reinforces shipped evidence.

## Conversion & proof

- Primary CTA: connect on LinkedIn. Secondary: browse the GitHub repos — the code keeps selling while they decide.
- The line a visitor remembers after 10 seconds: "The NUS CS student who actually ships."
- Belief ladder, ordered by evidence weight: internships first (MND, SCloud, NUS TA), then projects (NUSProfs, SafeNotes, coursemapped, and more), then activities (BrainHack 1st place 2026, Manus AI hackathon 1st place, NUS Fintech Summit), then films. The page's hierarchy should follow this order.
- Proof on hand: all inline on the site — internship roles with concrete outcomes ("reducing turnaround by 95%"), first-place hackathon finishes, public repos behind every project card, published short films.

## Brand Personality

Playful, technical, precise. The terminal-session aesthetic is the voice: real shell fluency (`whoami`, `cat experience.log`, `ls projects/`), dry humor in comments and emoji, and exacting craft in the details. The playfulness lands because the precision underneath it is genuine.

## Anti-references

- The template dev portfolio: hero headshot, skill bars, timeline, testimonial slider, generic Next.js/Tailwind scaffold.
- The corporate resume site: a LinkedIn profile re-rendered as a beige, formal, forgettable webpage.
- Overdone hacker cosplay: Matrix rain, glitch effects, fake hacking theatrics — the costume version of the terminal idea.
- Design-agency flash: heavy WebGL, huge display type, scroll-jacking — style that outshines the actual work.

## Design Principles

1. The terminal is native, not costume. Every shell detail must be something a real terminal user would recognize as correct; the moment it reads as decoration, it reads as cosplay.
2. Evidence over adjectives. The page persuades with shipped work — roles, wins, repos, outcomes — never with self-description.
3. Weight by evidence. Visual hierarchy and page order follow the belief ladder: internships > projects > activities > films.
4. Precision carries the joke. Humor (emoji, comments, "goes brrr") is earned by exact craft everywhere else — alignment, contrast, restrained motion.
5. The viewport is part of the design. Mobile responsiveness is a first-class requirement, not a breakpoint afterthought.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Mobile responsiveness is the explicit priority: the site must work and read well on phones, not just wide screens. Preserve the accessibility groundwork already in the code — reduced-motion alternatives for every animation, visible focus styles, sr-only section headings, and the no-JS-readable progressive enhancement approach.
